Minicom Setup#

Open the the plastic top. Had a nice serial port pins jutting out.

To communicate over Serial/UART you need minicom or picocom.

minicom -D \dev\ttyUSB0 -b 115200

breaking it down…

-D refers to the serial device interfcing with Netgear R7000. This is typically \dev\ttyUSB0 or \dev\ttyAMA0 or \dev\ttySerial0 If you troubled finding it, try dmesg | grep tty

-b refers to the baudrate of the device. typically declared by the manufacturer on their website. Minicom also recommends several preset options which should work 95% of the time.

R7000’s specs: baurdrate=115200; StopPins=8N1

Si Problemo: Pin Indentification + Troubleshooting#

Identifying the pins was quite annoying There is great article which describes how to indentify the serial pins. LINK A quick rundown is as follows:

  1. Identify GND: Set the Multimeter to Continuity mode / Lowest Ohm resisstance. Put one probe on the ground(usualy shielded components) and put the one on each pin. The pin with lowest resistace is the GND pin.

  2. Identify VCC: One probe on GND the other on each of the other pins. One will have a constant voltage of 3.3/5V. This is VCC pin

  3. Identify TX: One probe on GND the other on the remining 2. One will show fluctuating voltage. Try On/Off router. Some fluctuations will occur (unless pin is shorted)

  4. Identify RX: Lower voltage pin than TX. Might even be zero volts. But not as low resistance as GND

Always connect USB-to-TTL’s TX to Router’s RX and vice versa.

For Netgear R7000 specifically, don’t connect ground until the the router has booted up.

My Raspberry Pi 3 UART pins got fried during trial and error. I got a ESP-01S kit for $7 buck and use the provided USB-to-TTL CH340 serial convertor.
